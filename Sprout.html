Sprout your Ideas

Artistic creators, from dancers to painters, rely on inspiration and creativity in order to produce their work, but a major issue that artists run into is the dreaded art block. What do you do when you simply can’t come up with more ideas or lose motivation to finish a project you were previously so passionate about? While creativity is an abstract concept, my team of 3 wanted to tackle this challenge and provide artists with a tangible solution.

ROLE
UX Designer, Front-end Developer

TEAMMATES
Front-end Developer, Back-end Developer

CHALLENGE
Artistic creators need a tool to maintain their creativity in order to have ideas and finish a project.

SOLUTION
Sprout, a desktop app that organizes inspiration, references, and process of an artist's project.

Observations & Interviews

In order to better understand the creative process and what can lead to an art block, I began observing and interviewing people involved in different art forms. While dance and drawing are two very different mediums, we were able to find connections when simplifying their creative processes to the basics. While an individual’s work can come from their imagination, a lot of their creativity can be inspired by the physical world and their work can drastically change over time.

Observations allowed me to see how often the participants would reference physical objects and use tools to continue their work. It was when they ran out of ideas AND had no more references for inspiration that they felt demotivated. Creators needed a way to keep track of their process and references that inspired them in order to keep that motivation going.

Storyboards

To get a better sense of how we could target this need, we began storyboarding what a potential user’s creative process might look like if given a solution. This would give more insight on their behaviors and actions when a problem might occur.

While there were moments in their day where they had many ideas and couldn’t sit down to create them, there were other moments where they did have the time but no urge to create. We needed to fill that gap between moments of inspiration and the creation of art.

Lo-fi to Hi-fi Prototyping

With our storyboards, interviews, and observations to guide us, the next step was creating paper prototypes for the user interface. The creative art process is different for everyone, so how we framed our app would have to fit that unique experience while having a uniform design for multiple users.

User Testing

With our prototypes, we then began to do user testing to see if the functions we added were needed in the first place and if the task flows were intuitive. What we found was the appeal of the sprout page and that some functions were more intuitive than others. These notes helped us understand which functions we had to scaffold for the user and how to improve the user’s interactions with the app. Below are some examples of the changes we made.

One change we made was the navbar. We found that it was better to declutter the navbar by turning the "Plant Project" function into a popup rather than add it via the navbar. We also moved the function next to the home button so people would not mistakenly logout.

Another change was scaffolding the "Sprout leaf" function for new users. We found that most users tended to click on the instruction box rather than the button on the navbar. With this information, we decided to turn the description into the button itself. This also encourages people to continue to grow the treeline as the function as now a part of it like a growing tree.

Comparative Evaluations

To ensure that our redesign of the "Sprout Leaf" function was indeed needed, we tested the two versions to see which would be more intuitive for the users. With A/B testing, we found that the redesign decreases the time it take for the new user to click on the function, which was our goal.

Outcome

With a lot of changes made throughout the process, we were able to create a prototype that is now up and running for testing. You can plant a project and sprout with us too!

Reflection

With the topic that my team tackled, it allowed me to explore how the interfaces we use can also contribute the the mood and feelings that we experience while using them. This was especially important with the problem we were providing a solution for as many artists struggled with frustration that hindered their creativity. As the main UX designer, I was learned through many iterations how changing even a single word or location of a function could drastically change how a person. An example of this was the wording I used for a lot of the functions on the app. Instead of timeline, add, or file, words like treeline, sprout, or leaves encouraged the people to interact with the app more and increased their enjoyment with their projects as well. No matter how small the interactions the user might have with your app, you have the chance to make them all count. This project also showed me the importance to not assume what the user will do. A lot of the initial locations of the functions was placed due to our assumption on how users would use the app. Once we began to user test, we found that it was better to design our app to follow their path rather than reformatting our app to make them follow ours. There is a lot of insights you can get from user testing and observations if you are open to listen to them.
