A Hub for the LAB

Challenge
How can we help students find dance opportunities while increasing publicity for the Lab, UCSD's dance community commitee?

Tools
Personas, Wireframes, Figma

Role
Lead UX Designer, Researcher, Client Liaison

Solution

A site (mobile & desktop) that consolidates the different dimensions of dance opportunities in beginner friendly terminology while informing dancers of the LAB

Over 30 million people in the US have diabetes, and while it most often develops in adults, the decisions you make and habits you form in the early stages of your life can lead to prevention. Children are not always equipped with the knowledge to make healthy decisions though, especially children in poverty who might not have access to these choices. As members of Global Ties, a humanitarian program focused on solving socially urgent problems, our goal is to introduce this information in a way that is comprehensible, accessible, and fun to children.

Interviews & Personas

We first began to gather information about the Lab and their work so far while researching the dance community here at UCSD. With the information from our client interview, we defined our users into three possible categories: exploring dancers, dance team leadership, and dance legacies. This allowed us to account for new and current dancers, leadership, and dancers who have graduated but continue to dance at UCSD. We conducted user observations and interviews with dancers of various experiences and backgrounds. While we had initially believed that dance legacies would be a possible user, we found through interviews that they did not have many issues learning about the dance community because of their long history in the community and other priorities. This allowed us to prioritize exploring dancers and dance team leadership. We then created personas and scenarios they would run into if they tried to learn more about the dance community at UCSD.

From this information, we were able to dive deep into the situations in which their problems would stem from that we would not have seen before and create user cases for possible solutions to the problems they experienced. While some people had what looked like the same problems, their experiences diversified how they would find a solution and gave us insight on how we could scaffold those moments.

Competitive Analysis & Branding

While the Lab has existed for over 10 years, their prioritizing of the dance community meant that they had not focused on branding their organization. This meant that each year, their logos and designs would change and there was no consistency. This gave us both a lot of creative freedom and little direction to go with. Through competitive analysis of other sites that served similar functionality and research into the current dance community and trends, we gained key information on site architecture and functionality that would help dancers find what they need on our site. Below is the site architecture of the main pages of our site. We wanted to keep it simple to understand for new dancers and be action oriented when encouraging people to join the Lab.

With the current dance trends and themes we researched, we also created multiple moodboards to help with the branding of the Lab. Discussing the options with our client, we decided on the Lab brand: dramatic, sleek, dynamic, and science theme. This would allow us to focus on visuals of the dance community through the bold juxtaposition with the dark purple backgrounds. This focus on movement and the people themselves creates a welcoming vibe and highlights the most important part of dance: movement. We also wanted to create the distinction that this dance community is at UCSD, a major STEM college, via the science theme. Below are examples of moodboards I created.

Prototyping & Testing

We went through multiple iterations of the wireframe and prototypes with feedback from different levels of dancers at each step. This was really important when it came to new dancers who weren't sure of what certain dance terms meant or what the Lab actually was.

While we had a big focus on how users could discover dance teams and events at UCSD, we also wanted to be just as impactful in encouraging people to learn more about the Lab. This indirect method changed how we structured the site as we would leave trails for people to follow and ensure our wording were direct calls to action depending on which page they decided to navigate. Due to the amount of information that needed to be on this site, we had to implement methods of UX writing that would immediately pull people to important information on each page. Below are examples of the tweaks we made to highlight certain information and make it look more appealing to those on the site.

One of the hurdles we did not expect was the homepage itself. Due to the name of the organization, a majority of our user testers were confused as to what the Lab was. Their slogan “Like a chem lab, but for dance.” was not as straightforward at describing what the organization was, especially for new dancer. Through multiple iterations and redesigning of the homepage, we were able to create a statement that encompass much of what the Lab represented in a few words. We also included an event scrollable timeline on the homepage in order to actively engage people to explore events after calling them to dance with us.

Outcome

The Lab was very satisfied with our final design. The site would be able to help guide new and current dancers in finding where they belong in the dance community, level the playing field for dance teams in exposure, help dance teams communicate with each other, and increase publicity of the Lab. Through our branding, people can see that there are not only dance teams at UCSD, but a thriving dance community. Feel free to explore our mobile and desktop design for the Lab site!

Reflection

As someone who has led a dance team at UCSD myself, this was a great opportunity to give back to a community I am very passionate about. This project allowed me to take a step back from my experiences, see the journeys others have taken, and see how much potential the UCSD dance community has. Through the multiple iterations that we went through, I learned the importance of really knowing the industry you are working with in order to tap into that potential. While we could design a site that might look outwardly beautiful, if we do not create a site that will serve the needs of our users, our site will not have truly fulfilled its purpose. While we all had many great ideas for the site, honing in on what would best fit our audience was sometimes very difficult when we had to account for multiple user perspectives. It taught me the importance of continuously reiterating and to not be afraid of those changes. You might find that you go in circles or your previous idea was better, but designing is not a linear path. Going through that process shows you all the possibilities rather than failures that you can have until you land on one that fits while accounting for previous scenarios you might not have thought about before. My team especially was great to work with when it came to this. Sharing ideas with each other, being open to new ideas, voicing our opinions and critiques, this was essential in getting us to our final design.
