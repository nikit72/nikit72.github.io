A Hub for the LAB

CONTEXT
Established 10 years ago, the LAB was created to serve and unify the dance community at University of California, San Diego. While that has stayed as their mission over the years, present day has found the LAB in a precarious situation as the community they serve has grown from the 100's to 1000’s.

The LAB hosts multiple annual events to highlight dance teams, and dedicates themselves to bringing dancers together. While dancers perform at their events and use their main communication tool, a Facebook group, to share information and stay up to date, the lack of brand identity and the constraints in information organization has led to a disconnected community and a lack of recognition of the LAB among dancers.

ROLE
Lead UX Designer

TEAMMATES
Full Stack Developer, 2 UX Researchers

CHALLENGE
How can we improve the LAB's communication of resources to dancers in order fulfill the LAB's mission statement and increase recruitment rates?

SOLUTION
Creative brief for the LAB’s brand identity & high fidelity prototype (mobile & desktop) of a responsive site to provide dancers organized resources & improve the LAB's visibility

What's Not Working?

To better understand the issues dancers faced and the solutions the LAB already had, we gathered responses via surveys and conducted 9 in-depth interviews of the LAB staff members and both prospective and experienced dancers. We wanted to know how dancers currently found information about dance, what problems they were facing, and how aware they were of the LAB. Communication and organization was found to be a big issue as the LAB was not able to scale with the dance community over time.

29% had no reliable source for current dance events.

Of those who did, 100% had prior connections to dancers in the community already.

73% had frustrations advertising their teams and events.

There was no organized place to share information, allowing announcements to go unseen.

77% could not give a substantial answer of what the LAB did.

But they were able to recognize the LAB's annual events or resources by name.

In order to better serve the areas of needs that we discovered from our interviews, we categorized our audience into 3 user types: LAB staff, exploring dancer, and dance team leadership. Via personas, we immersed ourselves in the emotional nuances people experienced as they faced their problems. While we knew people didn't know where to look for dance information, personas allowed us to really think about the anxiety a novice dancer might feel when going to their first event or the insecurities a dancer might be hiding as they pursue a new team.

With these journeys in mind, we then developed key use cases in which a website would indeed solve people’s problems and used card sorting to visualize our users’ mental model of how information would be grouped. While the LAB did request a website, our research was able to back the decision that a site would be a scalable solution to the LAB’s issues and the growing problems of the dancers. People needed a novice friendly resource that consolidated information about the dance community, and this resource would inform them of the people who were doing this work as well.

Ideation

With a sense of how the information architecture of the site should look, we began ideating the structure of the site. As the mobile version would be the most simplified version of the site, we began with sketches of the mobile site. Brand Identity

Once we had a solid wireframe down, the next step was exploring the LAB’s branding. Based on current dance trends and the LAB’s loose theme, we created multiple moodboards to help visualize a new brand identity. Below are moodboards I created, with the first one being chosen for the LAB’s official brand identity.

Discussing the options with our client, we decided dramatic, sleek, dynamic, and science would best represent who they are. This would allow us to highlight dance visuals, creating a sense of awe and emphasizing the most important parts of dance: movement and the people. These beautiful visuals would then pop through bold juxtaposition with dark purple backgrounds. We also wanted to create the distinction that this dance community is at UCSD, a major STEM university, via the science theme.

Prototyping

Once in the prototyping phase, users began giving us more in depth feedback on the content on the site. One of the hurdles we experienced was the text on the site. Due to the name of the organization, a majority of our user testers were confused as to what the Lab was. Through multiple iterations of the homepage, we were able to create a statement that encompasses much of what the Lab represented in a few words.

As we prototyped the mobile site, we began prototyping out the desktop site as well. This allowed us to explore different dimensions and functions only possible on a desktop.

Outcome

The Lab was very satisfied with our final design. The site would be able to help guide new and current dancers in finding where they belong in the dance community, level the playing field for dance teams in exposure, help dance teams communicate with each other, and increase publicity of the Lab. Through our branding, people can see that there are not only dance teams at UCSD, but a thriving dance community. Feel free to explore our mobile and desktop design for the Lab site!
