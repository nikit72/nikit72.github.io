Diabeatit

Challenge
How can we teach healthy behaviors to kids as a means of type 2 diabetes prevention?

Tools
User Research & Testing,
Prototyping, Figma

Role
UX Design Team Lead
Client Liaison

Solution

An engaging & educational children's game about the causes, effects, and management of Type 2 Diabetes

Over 30 million people in the US have diabetes, and while it most often develops in adults, the decisions you make and habits you form in the early stages of your life can lead to prevention. Children are not always equipped with the knowledge to make healthy decisions though, especially children in poverty who might not have access to these choices. As members of Global Ties, a humanitarian program focused on solving socially urgent problems, our goal is to introduce this information in a way that is comprehensible, accessible, and fun to children.

How do Kids Learn?

This was one of our biggest questions at the start of this project. While the previous team had created a prototype, we found it lacked gameplay backed by effective learning models. After talking to educational professionals who work with children, I looked into the California Department of Education’s Health Education Content Standards to see how our material would align with each grade’s current curriculum.

Caren Holtzman from the UCSD PAL Program had mentioned the developmental differences for the age range 8-13 were too vast. Narrowing our scope to 4th and 5th graders would allow us to apply teaching methods that would prove effective in developing this group’s problem solving and decision making skills for their well being. We discovered the five step decision process, used in CDE’s Health Education Framework, would be a useful tool in formatting how kids can learn through our minigames.

Evaluating Current Game

Another aspect of the game that needed to be developed was the user interface of the game itself. While we wanted to teach kids about diabetes, we also had to make sure that kids would want to play and could navigate the game to begin with. This meant analyzing the different parts of the game to see what was lacking and what was working to better improve the play experience.

A New Game Narrative

In order for kids to stay engaged while learning through the game, we wanted to create a new narrative for the game. Rather than having an introduction where a kid with diabetes comes home from school and then has access to multiple minigames, I explored a different approach that followed a make-believe story but allowed kids to still make connections to real life applications.

By personifying aspects of diabetes, such as Gluccy the Glucose, the player would be able to assist Gluccy in their journey through a human body that has type 2 diabetes. This would allow us to create minigames that follow a storyline and add imaginative and interactive details to the game that would not be available if grounded in reality.

Ideating Lesson Plan Structurese

In order to test out our concepts based on our research, we wanted to have at least one game structured based on the Health Education Frameworks. Due to time restraints, we decided to restructure a current minigame in Diabeatit called Kitchen Showdown. Its current tutorial is filled with a lot of text, making it very easy to skip by children. While the game itself can be very informative when playing, it is also very possible for the player to gloss over it for the functions of the gameplay itself. To begin, we first started to ideate different lesson plan models that the game could follow.

We also redesigned the user interface of the game to include affordances that would help kids navigate the game.

User Testing

With a prototype created and ready for testing, we connected with teachers in the greater San Diego area and were lucky enough to be able to test the game with fourth grade students. Because we wanted to test retention of new knowledge, we created a quiz about diabetes for before and after the game. We then allowed them to play through it and voice their thoughts and opinions. It was a really good experience hearing from kids themselves and their suggestions on how to make the game better! We gained a lot of insights and found that the children’s knowledge of diabetes did increase by the end of the game. With that, we analyzed the data and will be passing on our work to the next team.
