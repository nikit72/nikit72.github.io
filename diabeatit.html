DIABEATIT

Background

Over 30 million people in the US have diabetes, and while it most often develops in adults, the decisions you make and the habits you form in the early stages of your life can lead to the prevention of type 2 diabetes. Anupam and Vidya are graduate students in the medical sciences at UCSD who are passionate about spreading health awareness to the public. As a member of Global Ties, a program that partners with nonprofits and NGOs to co-create solutions to socially urgent problems, our team was tasked with creating an engaging and comprehensive game for kids to learn about type 2 diabetes prevention. After completion of the game, it will be hosted in hospital waiting rooms for kids to play. With 2 years contributed to this project by previous members already, our team is taking the work and research that many conducted and continuing to iterate.

Lesson Plan Research

While the previous team had passed on a setup for the game, the current version lacked the backing of effective learning models intertwined with the gameplay and the age range 8-13 for the game was too broad to account for all the developmental differences. After talking to professionals who work with children, we looked into the Health Education Content Standards and current curriculum for each grade to see how our material would align with what students were already learning. This allowed us to narrow our scope to fourth and fifth graders, who would be learning material that aligned with what Diabeatit would teach.

With this, Our team looked into how our target age group learned in order to apply teaching methods that would prove effective in developing their problem solving and decision making for their well being. We discovered the five step decision process would be a useful tool in formatting how kids can learn through our minigames.

Game Design

Another aspect of the game that needed to be developed was the design of the game itself. While we wanted to teach kids about diabetes prevention and management, we had to make sure that kids would want to play the game in the first place. This meant analyzing the different parts of the game to see what was lacking and what was working to better improve the play experience.

Creating a Narrative

In order for kids to stay engaged in the game in a healthy manner, we revamped how the structure of the game flowed. Rather than the game following the life of a kid with diabetes come home from school, I explored a different narrative that was more whimsical but allowed kids to still make connections to real life application.

Minigame Structure & Prototype

The current tutorial for the minigame tutorial is filled with a lot of information about diabetes, and that’s what makes it easily skippable by children. We broke down the information into easy to understand dialogue and formed a story with facts about diabetes based on effective lesson plan structures. When creating a prototype, we redesigned the interface to better fit the interactions needed for kids to be drawn to the correct information on the screen. Feel free to explore our prototype for Kitchen Showdown!

User Testing

With a prototype created and ready for testing, we connected with teachers in the greater San Diego area and were lucky enough to be able to test the game with fourth grade students. Because we wanted to test retention of new knowledge, we created a quiz about diabetes for before and after the game. We then allowed them to play through it and voice their thoughts and opinions. It was a really good experience hearing from kids themselves and their suggestions on how to make the game better! We gained a lot of insights and found that the children’s knowledge of diabetes did increase by the end of the game. With that, we analyzed the data and will be passing on our work to the next team.
